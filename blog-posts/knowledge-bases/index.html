Queries over Incomplete Knowledge Bases

General background knowledge about the world is a significant component in many AI systems. A paradigmatic instance of such a system is a question answering system (ex. Siri). Question answering aims to answer, potentially complex, questions about any topic in a broad domain such as:

  • Who won the Super Bowl last year?
  • How old is President Biden?
  • What is the oldest breed of dog?

Luckily, due to sharing of information on the internet, knowledge is extremely accessible. Large amounts of knowledge are typically stored in a structure similar to a graph database. Represented as a relational graph, this structure of general background knowledge is called a knowledge base. Knowledge bases have achieved advances in question answering, but inherently suffer from incompleteness. It is impossible to store all knowledge in a ever changing world in such a structure. Missing nodes or edges create challenges in querying the graph for knowledge.

In this blog post, I will discuss \textit{Reasoning With Neural Tensor Networks for Knowledge Base Completion} \cite{NTN}, a method to perform inference over missing edges. However, in my full report LINK HERE, I provide a more complete survey of \textsc{Queries over Incomplete Knowledge Bases} and summarize the aformentioned paper along with a second paper: \textit{Embedding Logical Queries on Knowledge Graphs} \cite{conjunctive} which discusses a method to reason over missing relations through conjunctive logical queries.

Consider the example knokwledge base subgraph surrounding OJ Simpson, an NFL running back notorious for his arrest and historic criminal trial.

I re-evaluate these works on Wikidata, a modern collaboratively edited dataset. Wikidata presents challenges due to its size and sparsity. Predominantly because of sparsity, I found that the performance on Wikidata suffered for both techniques. The performance was slightly worse, but comparable, to the datasets on which they were originally evaluated. Interestingly, with an adjustment in the node embeddings to handle sparsity, \textit{Reasoning With Neural Tensor Networks for Knowledge Base Completion}, achieved an \textit{improvement} in performance over the original evaluation. Both methods proved to be reasonable approaches for performing queries over incomplete modern databases.
